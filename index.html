My story

Two years in rehab clinics before I wrote a single line of code.

400+ hours of clinical observation watching patients interact with medical devices. I saw the disconnect. Engineers designing in isolation, safety reports that missed real-world use patterns, and a review process that hadn't changed in decades.

So I went to Stevens for my M.S. in Biomedical Engineering. Got my Google UX Design Certificate because the gap between what gets built and what patients experience is a design problem. Then I started building UseTrace HFE, an AI system that handles the tedious classification work so the expert can focus on making the device safer.

I'm not here because AI is trending. I'm here because I spent two years watching the problem and couldn't stop thinking about it. (My friends can confirm. I'm not great at dinner parties anymore.)

Featured

UseTrace HFE

AI classification for medical device usability observations. The expert reviews. The AI drafts. Nobody cuts corners on patient safety.

Building now

AI-powered human factors analysis

Classifies observations per IEC 62366-1 and FDA HFE guidance. Structured inputs in, reviewer-ready draft out.

The problem

Safety engineers manually classify hundreds of usability observations per study. In Excel. In 2026. It takes 2-4 weeks. Consistency across reviewers is low. There's no validated AI tool for it.

What I'm building

Takes study evidence (task lists, observer notes, device context) and classifies each observation with confidence scores and reasoning traces. The engineer validates a draft instead of starting from scratch.
