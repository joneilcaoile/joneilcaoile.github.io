Pneumothorax Air Leak Monitor

A ventilator-integrated device that estimates air leak severity objectively. Where clinicians currently guess, this device measures. Regulatory-aware, V&V-first medical device design.

The clinical problem

Pneumothorax — a collapsed lung — requires chest tube drainage on mechanically ventilated patients. Once the tube is placed, the question becomes: when is the air leak resolved enough to remove it?

Currently, clinicians assess leak severity by watching a water seal chamber. They observe bubbling and estimate: "More bubbles = worse leak. Fewer bubbles = better." There's no quantitative standard. Two clinicians on different shifts make different judgments about the same patient. Some chest tubes stay in longer than needed. Others come out too early.

The standard of care is subjective, qualitative, and inconsistent. An objective measurement system could change clinical outcomes.

The Current Approach

Visual assessment of bubbling in water seal chamber. Subjective severity grading. No standardized criteria. Inconsistent decisions across shifts and clinicians. Longer-than-necessary chest tube placement or premature removal.

Our Solution

Continuous, objective air leak quantification by comparing ventilator-delivered tidal volume with chest tube outflow. Algorithm outputs a severity score. Clinicians have data to drive weaning decisions. Measurable outcomes. Regulated, validated approach.

Why this matters

Unnecessary chest tube days cost money and increase infection risk. Every extra day of a chest tube is a day of infection risk, a day the patient can't mobilize fully, a day of resource use. Conversely, removing a tube too early can lead to re-collapse and re-insertion.

Objective data drives clinical confidence. When a clinician has a quantitative leak score trending downward, they can confidently wean. When the score plateaus or rises, they know to investigate further. The decision becomes evidence-based, not guesswork.

This is a real regulatory gap. No FDA-cleared device exists for objective pneumothorax air leak monitoring. That's an opportunity — and a responsibility to get it right.

System architecture

The device sits between the ventilator circuit and the patient's chest tube. It measures flow/volume on both sides and computes a leak severity score in real time.

Key design principles

Dual-point sensing. We measure tidal volume delivered to the patient (ventilator side) and air volume exiting the chest tube (drain side). The difference is the leak. No estimates, no guessing — direct measurement.

Microcontroller-based processing. Real-time computation ensures the clinician sees the leak score update continuously, not in batches. Responsive to changing clinical conditions.

Validated algorithm. The leak severity score isn't arbitrary. It's derived from first principles (volume balance) and validated against simulated test cases and benchtop prototypes. Every output has a clear, auditable reasoning path.

Regulatory-first design. From day one, this device is designed to meet IEC 60601-1, ISO 14971, and 21 CFR 820. Not an afterthought. Not "we'll worry about compliance later." It's built in.

Why these choices?

Dual gas/pressure sensors. Not one sensor. Two — one inlet-side, one drain-side — for redundancy and confidence. In medical devices, redundancy isn't paranoia. It's design.

STM32 microcontroller. Proven in medical devices. Good real-time performance, extensive manufacturer documentation, and a strong ecosystem for safety-critical firmware.

SolidWorks CAD. Industry standard in medtech. Every supplier, every manufacturer knows it. Makes communication and iteration fast.

Benchtop-first validation. Before anything touches a patient, we'll have tested this system hundreds of times with simulated pneumothorax conditions. Simulated-use testing is our bridge between design and clinical reality.

What this design taught me

Standards aren't red tape — they're design constraints that improve the product. IEC 60601-1 isn't a box to check at the end. It shapes every choice: component derating, failure mode analysis, display design. Better standards lead to better devices.

The hardest part of medical device design is asking the right clinical questions. Sensing is straightforward. Microcontrollers are commodity. The hard part is: What exactly needs to be measured? What's the clinical decision boundary? How will clinicians use this thing in a chaos-filled ICU? Getting those answers right changes everything downstream.

Objective measurement changes clinical behavior. When a clinician has a number instead of a guess, they make better decisions. They're faster, more confident, more consistent. That's the real value of this device.

V&V testing is not a phase at the end. It's woven into the design. We're not designing the device first and then figuring out how to test it. We're designing the test (simulated use) in parallel, which forces us to think about every edge case early.
