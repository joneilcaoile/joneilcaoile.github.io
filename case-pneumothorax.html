The Clinical Problem

A real gap with no commercial solution

When a pneumothorax patient is on a ventilator, a chest tube drains the leaked air from the pleural space. Clinicians need to know how severe the leak is to decide when it's safe to remove the tube. The current method: watch the bubbling in the drainage chamber and make a subjective judgment.

There's no standardized, objective measurement. No device on the market measures pulmonary air leak severity continuously and quantitatively. Patients stay on chest tubes longer than necessary because there's no clear data-driven threshold to trigger a weaning decision. This means more hospital days, more infection risk, and more discomfort for no clinical benefit.

Current state
Visual assessment, no standard threshold

Clinicians assess air leak severity by watching a water-seal chamber. The observation is categorical at best, subjective in practice. No measurement. No trend data. No objective weaning criteria.

Our approach
Continuous, quantified leak severity

By comparing ventilator-delivered tidal volume with measured chest tube outflow, we can compute a leak differential: a real number that can be trended over time and used as a weaning trigger.

Device Architecture

Two measurement points. One differential.

The core idea is straightforward: measure what the ventilator delivers and what escapes through the chest tube. The difference is the leak. We use gas flow and pressure sensors at both points, feed the signals into a microcontroller, and compute the differential in real time.

Key Design Decisions

Comparative measurement over absolute measurement

We don't need to know the exact air leak volume. We need to know how much of what the ventilator sent didn't stay in the lungs. A differential approach is more robust to sensor drift and calibration variance than trying to measure absolute outflow, and it maps directly to the clinical question: how much is leaking?

Bench prototype scope: by design

BME 565 is a device design course, not a clinical trial. We scoped the prototype to demonstrate the sensing principle and computation without attempting patient contact. A bench-level prototype that proves the measurement concept is the appropriate deliverable at this stage. The right next step would be animal model validation, not human use.

Simulated-use workflow first

Before touching hardware, I designed the simulated-use workflow: the sequence of steps a clinician would follow to set up, use, and interpret the device. Starting from use first rather than specs first kept the design anchored to what the user actually needs, not just what we could build.

Clinical Integration: How ICU Teams Would Use This

Current weaning decisions rely on subjective assessment: clinicians visually inspect the chest tube water seal chamber for bubbling, then guess whether the leak is resolving. This device replaces that guess with a number. The clinical workflow changes from "check for bubbles every 4 hours" to "read the leak differential on the bedside display and compare against the threshold the attending set at admission."

Task analysis identified 5 critical interaction points: initial sensor attachment to the ventilator circuit, chest tube sensor placement, threshold calibration by the attending physician, routine reading by bedside nurses (every 2-4 hours), and the weaning decision checkpoint. Each interaction was evaluated for potential use errors: sensor misalignment, threshold confusion between patients, alarm fatigue from false positives, and one-handed operation constraints during emergencies.

The IEC 62366-1 use specification was drafted before hardware prototyping. Constraints it surfaced: the display must be readable from 6 feet (nurse station distance), alarms must distinguish from existing ventilator alarms, and the sensor attachment must be completable in under 30 seconds by a single nurse.

Supporting Clinicians After Deployment

The deployment plan includes a 30-minute bedside training protocol for ICU nurses covering sensor attachment, threshold interpretation, and alarm response. A one-page troubleshooting guide addresses the three most common field issues: sensor displacement during patient repositioning, false alarms from ventilator circuit disconnection, and threshold confusion between patients (each patient gets an individually calibrated baseline). Clinician feedback feeds back to engineering through a structured observation form: nurses log discrepancies between device output and their clinical assessment, creating a continuous validation loop.

Sensing at both ends of the airway

Gas flow and pressure sensors are placed at the ventilator circuit (inlet) and at the chest tube (outflow). The microcontroller computes the differential between delivered volume and measured outflow, which represents the volume leaking into the pleural space. Two measurement points, one clinically meaningful number.

CAD and form factor planning

Form factor matters for a bedside ICU device. The enclosure needs to be cleanable, mountable to a ventilator pole or bed rail, and interpretable at a glance by a nurse who's managing multiple patients. Early CAD work focused on those constraints before finalizing the sensing architecture. A technically correct sensor in an unusable housing doesn't get adopted.

Design Iteration

Three major pivots from concept to final prototype

Iteration 1: Single Airflow Sensor
The Problem: Initial design used a single thermal mass airflow sensor. It showed good sensitivity at low flows (10–20 mL/min) but saturated above 150 mL/min, making it impossible to quantify clinically significant leaks.
What Failed: After running tests on a bench fixture with reference flows up to 200 mL/min, the sensor output plateaued. We could detect a leak was present, but not how severe it was. That ambiguity is unusable in a clinical setting.
Next Step: Added a differential pressure channel. This gives us two independent measurements. The airflow sensor handles low-flow sensitivity, pressure sensor handles the dynamic range.
Iteration 2: Dual Sensors with Fusion
The Problem: With two sensors, we got 4x dynamic range (now 10–200 mL/min measurable), but the alarm latency shot up to 8 seconds. Two separate thresholds meant waiting for both to agree before declaring a leak. The 8-second lag was clinically unacceptable.
What Failed: Simple threshold logic treats the sensors independently. If either crosses 150 mL/min, alarm fires. But noise on one sensor creates false alarms (2.3% false positive rate). We needed to weight the measurements intelligently.
Next Step: Implemented a Kalman filter to fuse the two measurements. The filter weights the airflow sensor heavily at low flows (where it's accurate) and shifts trust to the pressure sensor at high flows. Alarm latency dropped to 2.3s and false positives fell to 2%.
Iteration 3: Sensor Fusion with State Estimation
The Problem: Final concern was robustness during transients. When a nurse manually adjusts the chest tube or a patient coughs, the pressure signal spikes briefly. The Kalman filter was catching these as false positives (about 10% of ICU-simulated scenarios).
What Failed: The filter's process noise and measurement noise covariances were tuned for steady-state operation. Sudden transients violated those assumptions, so the filter briefly lost confidence in both sensors and triggered alarms.
Final Design: Added a transient detector that temporarily increases the filter's measurement noise window during pressure changes faster than 20 cmH2O/s. This de-weights the pressure sensor during the spike, then re-converges once the transient clears. Final false positive rate: 2/100 trials.

What I've Learned So Far

The gap between concept and prototype is mostly process

01
The literature review changed what we thought we were building.

Before I touched any hardware or CAD, I spent time in the literature on air leak monitoring methods: existing research, tried approaches, why they didn't become commercial products. That review moved us away from our first-pass concept (simple flow meter at the chest tube) toward the differential approach. The hard design decisions got easier once we understood why no one had solved this with a simpler method.

02
Starting from use specification, not engineering specs, keeps a device design honest.

The instinct in an engineering course is to start with what you can build and work backward to a use case. I pushed us to define the simulated-use workflow first: who uses this, in what environment, under what time pressure, with what prior training? That kept us from designing a device that would work in a lab but not in an ICU. The use specification flagged constraints (alarm management, cleanability, one-handed operation) that would never appear in a component-level spec.

03
Risk management isn't a document you write at the end.

Working through ISO 14971 risk analysis during the design phase (not after) caught at least two design choices that could have created hazardous situations. Sensor failure modes, alarm conditions, and edge cases in the differential computation aren't obvious until you ask: what happens if this fails, and who gets hurt? Running that analysis early changed the design, not just the documentation.
