The problem nobody talks about

Every medical device that touches a patient goes through usability testing before market clearance. A trained safety engineer reviews the test recordings, classifies every observation — was it a use error? A close call? A difficulty? — and maps each one to regulatory categories defined by IEC 62366-1 and FDA Human Factors guidance.

They do this in Excel. For hundreds of observations. One at a time. It takes 2–4 weeks per study. Inter-rater reliability across different reviewers hovers around 0.60 — barely above "moderate agreement." The process hasn't changed in decades.

Before UseTrace

Manual classification in Excel spreadsheets. One observation at a time. 2–4 weeks per study. No consistency checks. No reasoning trail. Reviewer fatigue leads to missed patterns.

With UseTrace

Structured input → AI draft classification with confidence scores and reasoning traces → expert validation. Hours instead of weeks. Every decision has a paper trail. The engineer focuses on judgment, not data entry.

How it works

UseTrace takes structured study evidence — task lists, observer notes, device context — and produces reviewer-ready classification drafts. The engineer validates instead of starting from scratch.

Key design decisions

Pydantic for data integrity. Medical device data has zero tolerance for malformed inputs. Every observation passes through strict schema validation before the AI sees it. If a field is missing or invalid, it fails loudly — not silently.

Confidence scores on every classification. The AI doesn't just say "use error" — it gives a confidence score and a reasoning trace. Low-confidence items get flagged for careful human review. High-confidence items still need sign-off, but the cognitive load drops dramatically.

Human-in-the-loop, always. This isn't a black box that replaces the expert. It's a drafting tool. The safety engineer reviews every classification. The difference is they're reviewing a structured draft with reasoning, not staring at a blank Excel cell.

Regulatory foundation

This isn't a side project — it's built on the actual standards that govern medical device usability worldwide. Every classification category maps directly to regulatory taxonomy.

IEC 62366-1:2015

International standard for medical device usability engineering. Defines the 4 observation categories UseTrace classifies into.

FDA HFE Guidance

FDA's 2016 guidance on applying human factors to medical devices. Shapes how observations map to risk analysis.

IEC 62366-2

Technical report with practical guidance for implementing usability engineering processes.

21 CFR 820 (QMSR)

Quality Management System Regulation. UseTrace's output format is designed to slot directly into QMS documentation.

Why these choices?

FastAPI + Pydantic — Type safety for medical data. Auto-generated API docs. Async for handling multiple concurrent review sessions.

Claude API — Best-in-class reasoning for nuanced classification tasks. The difference between "use error" and "close call" often comes down to intent, context, and clinical judgment — not keyword matching.

React dashboard — Reviewers need to scan, filter, override, and export. A responsive UI beats Excel for the validation workflow.

What I learned

Regulatory documents are a design constraint, not a checkbox. IEC 62366-1's observation categories aren't just labels — they carry specific implications for risk analysis, design changes, and post-market surveillance. Getting a classification wrong doesn't just affect a spreadsheet. It affects whether a device gets cleared.

AI confidence and human confidence aren't the same. A model can be 95% confident and still wrong in ways that matter clinically. That's why UseTrace always surfaces its reasoning — so the human reviewer can catch the cases where high confidence masks flawed logic.

The hardest part isn't the AI. It's the data model. Getting the Pydantic schemas right — so that observations, device context, task context, and regulatory categories all relate correctly — took longer than the classification engine. Good data architecture makes everything downstream easier.
